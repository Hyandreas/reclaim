# 1. Covenant Chain — the agent that catches the amendment everyone forgot to re-check

**One-line pitch:** A web-based enterprise agent that reads a credit agreement the way a covenant analyst should but rarely has time to: it plans which covenants apply, retrieves the governing ratio definition, and recomputes the ratio itself with a calculator tool (zero LLM arithmetic). When its own number doesn't match the borrower's self-reported figure, that *mismatch is the trigger* — not a script — for a second retrieval into the deal's amendment history, to check whether "Consolidated EBITDA" has been quietly redefined since the base agreement was signed. It resolves which version of the contract actually controls, re-verifies the driver against the transaction ledger, and ends in a cited, audit-ready escalation memo — every figure traceable to the exact clause, base agreement or amendment, that produced it — in under a minute.

# 3. Problem & who it's for (usefulness / relevance)

**The exact user:** a covenant / credit-risk analyst at a private-credit fund or a bank's leveraged-lending desk.

**The pain, precisely:** every quarter, that analyst re-derives covenant compliance by cross-referencing an 80–150 page credit agreement's defined terms against the borrower's latest financials. The obvious failure mode — the one every "chat with the credit agreement" build targets — is a buried carve-out. The *less obvious, more expensive* failure mode is structural: credit agreements are living documents. A facility signed in year one routinely picks up a First Amendment, a covenant-relief side letter, an Incremental Facility Amendment — each one capable of silently redefining "Consolidated EBITDA," "Total Net Debt," or a threshold itself. A tired analyst who re-reads the *original* agreement every quarter — which is what most manual reviews actually do, because nobody re-diffs the full amendment stack each cycle — is computing the covenant against a definition that may no longer be the one in force. This doesn't look wrong when it happens; it looks like an ordinary quarterly breach (or, worse, an ordinary quarterly pass), which is exactly why it survives review.

**Why it's real, not hypothetical:** firms already pay outside research vendors (Covenant Review, Reorg) to track precisely this — which documents amend which definitions, and which version currently governs a given facility. That proves both the buyer and the willingness to spend on solving it.

**The value:** compress the quarterly ritual into a cited, auditable, sub-minute check that reads the *entire* amendment history, not just the document an analyst happens to have open — with every figure clickable back to its source page, base agreement or amendment. A credit committee could act on the output directly.

**Why finance and not the other three verticals:** finance is the only one where "recompute your own arithmetic, and let a disagreement with the counterparty's number trigger you to go check whether you're even reading the current contract" is the *natural shape* of the workflow rather than something bolted on. The ground truth — the ratio, and which document was filed later — is arithmetic and dates, both independently checkable facts, which makes the demo bulletproof to live judge challenges (see §8).

# 4. What it does — the idea (concrete)

The analyst opens a one-borrower portfolio view. **Meridian Logistics** — our on-screen pseudonym for a real, EDGAR-sourced borrower we hand-select and validate on Day 0, so the underlying legal text and figures are genuine, not invented (honest disclosure in §7) — is flashing amber ("cushion shrinking"). They click it, and the agent runs live, on screen:

1. **PLANS** which covenants are tested this quarter (from the covenant schedule) — and flags which of those covenants' defined terms have amendment history on file and therefore need a version check before anything is computed. A covenant whose definition was never amended skips the check entirely: a real decision made from the document set, not a static list-pull.
2. **RETRIEVES (pass 1 — base agreement):** the exact Net Leverage *definition* and its carve-out / permitted add-back language from the original credit agreement — not just the number, the exceptions.
3. **RETRIEVES (financials):** the raw line items the definition requires (Total Net Debt, EBITDA).
4. **CALLS A TOOL:** a deterministic `calculate_ratio` function recomputes Net Leverage from the raw line items under the base-agreement definition. **The LLM never does the arithmetic.**
5. **DECIDES — and a disagreement is the trigger, not a script:** recomputed 4.08x vs. threshold 4.00x → **BREACH on the base definition.** But the borrower's *self-reported* figure is 3.95x — a **+0.13x discrepancy**, more than six times past the fixed 0.02x tolerance that would otherwise wave off ordinary rounding noise. Rather than assume the counterparty is simply wrong, the agent treats an unexplained gap between its own math and theirs as a reason to check its own assumptions first — starting with *which version of the contract it just read.*
6. **RETRIEVES AGAIN (pass 2 — amendment history) and RESOLVES PRECEDENCE, conditionally:** because the gap is unexplained, it searches the agreement's amendment corpus and finds a **First Amendment, filed 18 months after the base agreement**, that expanded the EBITDA add-back basket for one-time legal and restructuring charges — a different PDF, a different page. A small deterministic check (not a model opinion) compares filing dates: the amendment post-dates the base agreement and is not itself superseded, so **it controls.**
7. **RETRIEVES A THIRD TIME (transaction ledger) and RECOMPUTES:** pulls the quarter's GL, finds a **$4.2M one-time legal settlement**, and checks it against the *amendment's* — not the original agreement's — add-back criteria. It qualifies. Pro-forma Net Leverage recomputes to **3.88x → WATCH, not BREACH** — the gap to the borrower's self-report narrows from 0.13x to 0.07x, the residual the confidence score (§6) prices in rather than papers over.
8. **PRODUCES** a one-page escalation memo, writing itself sentence by sentence, where every figure is a clickable citation back to the exact page — base agreement, amendment, or GL — plus a **confidence score shown as a defined, weighted breakdown** (formula in §6, not a vibe).
9. **DEFERS TO A HUMAN:** Approve & Escalate / Override & Request More Data. On approve, a real Slack notification fires on stage.

The second and third retrievals aren't scheduled waypoints in a script — they exist because a number the agent computed didn't match a number its counterparty reported, and it went looking for why. That sequencing — **the discrepancy appears on screen before the retrieval it causes** — is the actual, legible difference between RAG and an agent, and we choreograph it to be unmissable.

# 5. Why it's novel / differentiation vs. the obvious approach

We're aware this sits close to the brief's own Finance example — "retrieve the credit agreement and prior filings to check trend... cross-checks recent transactions for a cause." That's not an accident to hide; it's the correct shape for this vertical, and half the room will build some version of it: a document chatbot, or a carve-out lookup with a calculator bolted on. What we push past the expected version on is the axis most of those builds won't touch:

- **It questions which document is even authoritative — not just what the document says.** A carve-out lookup treats "the credit agreement" as one static PDF. Real facilities aren't static: they accumulate amendments that silently redefine the very terms a ratio depends on. Covenant Chain doesn't just find the relevant clause — it checks whether a later, separately-filed document already changed it. That's a genuinely different retrieval problem than "find the paragraph," and it's the nuance a rushed analyst is *most* likely to miss completely, because missing an amendment means never even opening the document that has the real answer.
- **The second retrieval is triggered by a disagreement, not a schedule.** The agent doesn't check the amendment index because a script tells it to run that step after every ratio calculation — it checks because its own recomputed number didn't match the counterparty's self-reported figure, and an unexplained gap is exactly the kind of thing that should make an analyst (or an agent) doubt its own assumptions before doubting someone else's math. That is "retrieves more than once when it needs to," demonstrated rather than asserted, because the trigger is visible on screen *before* the retrieval it causes.
- **Two things are computed, not asserted — the ratio and the precedence call.** The ratio comes from deterministic Python. Which document controls comes from a filing-date comparison, also deterministic, never an LLM opinion. A judge can challenge either live — the number, or the "why the amendment wins" claim — and get a re-derivation, not a hedge. Most agent demos can only defend their arithmetic; we can also defend our bibliography.
- **Glass-box, not black-box.** A live agent trace plus a memo that cites every claim to its exact source page — base agreement, amendment, or ledger — directly answers the classic enterprise-AI adoption failure (IBM Watson for Oncology: recommendations professionals couldn't trust because they couldn't see the reasoning). Our citation-first, human-in-the-loop design is the adoption pitch in one sentence.

Versus incumbent research vendors (Covenant Review, Reorg): they sell human-tracked amendment analysis on a delay; we deliver a self-documenting, version-aware check in under a minute. Versus a same-track "chat with the credit agreement" build: we're checking a fact those builds don't even know to ask — *is this still the current definition?*

# 6. How it works — architecture & AGENTIC flow

**Stack (all on Vultr):**
- **Frontend:** Next.js — a three-zone "Glass Box" UI (portfolio → live trace → memo/decision). Consumes **Server-Sent Events** so every agent step renders live, not as a post-hoc replay. Citation chips carry a document-title badge (Base Agreement / First Amendment / GL) so a viewer can see, at a glance, when a claim's source just switched documents.
- **Backend:** Python + FastAPI. A **hand-rolled agent loop** — an explicit state machine, *not* LangGraph/AutoGen — with a hard step cap. (Live demos punish framework magic more than they reward it.)
- **Reasoning model:** **Claude Opus 4.8** (`claude-opus-4-8`, 1M context) via the Anthropic Messages API, driving planning, tool selection, clause interpretation, and memo drafting, with `thinking: {type: "adaptive"}` + `output_config: {effort: "high"}`. (`claude-sonnet-5` is the drop-in lower-latency/cost option — `claude-sonnet-5` is $3/$15 per 1M, intro $2/$10 through 2026-08-31 — if we need to shave demo seconds; the arithmetic and the precedence check never depend on the model, so quality is not at risk.) Track 2 (Vultr) mandates no specific model, so we use the strongest reasoner for the two hardest judgment calls in this workflow: interpreting carve-out language, and weighing whether a driver genuinely qualifies under a since-amended definition.
- **Retrieval:** the credit agreement **and each of its amendments** are chunked **by clause**, tagged with section, page, **document title, and effective date**, into **pgvector** (Vultr Managed Database) — so a retrieval can be scoped to the base agreement, the amendment set, or both, and every result carries which document it came from. Financials and the GL/transaction ledger stay as **plain structured Postgres tables** — not everything needs an embedding, and showing that judgment matters. Embeddings via a local sentence-transformer model hosted on the Vultr Compute instance (fully self-contained, zero external dependency for demo reliability); Voyage AI embeddings (Anthropic's recommended provider) are the higher-quality swap-in if time allows.
- **Precedence resolution — deterministic, not the model's call.** Once two candidate clauses for the same defined term come back (base + amendment), a small backend function compares effective dates and supersession flags and returns the controlling one. This is not a sixth tool the model invokes — it's backend logic that runs between retrieval and the agent's next step, following the same rule as the calculator: **if a fact is objectively checkable, code decides it, not the LLM.**
- **Grounding / citations:** two layers. (a) our own clause-chunk citations (document title + section + page) from pgvector for retrieved clauses; (b) **Claude's native Citations feature** (`citations: {enabled: true}` on document blocks, used on the memo's narrative call — see below), which returns `page_location` (1-indexed start/end page) so memo claims link to the exact PDF passage the model actually used, in whichever of the two PDFs it's in. Clicking a citation pill jumps to and highlights that page.
- **Confidence score — defined, not asserted.** A weighted blend of three sub-scores, each computed by backend code, never self-reported by the LLM: **document coverage (40%)** — required data points resolved with no missing-datum flags, as a percentage; **calc-match (30%)** — `100 × max(0, 1 − |recomputed − self-reported| / self-reported)`, i.e. how closely the agent's own math lands to the counterparty's number once the controlling definition is applied; **precedence certainty (30%)** — 100 if exactly one controlling document version was found with an unambiguous effective date, discounted for any additional candidate amendment needing disambiguation or any closing-condition language that leaves the effective date itself uncertain. Worked example from the demo run: coverage 100 → 40.0 pts; calc-match ≈98 (3.88x recomputed vs. 3.95x self-reported) → 29.5 pts; precedence certainty 75 (one controlling amendment found, minor closing-condition caveat) → 22.5 pts — **total = 92.** A human always sees the memo regardless of score (§4, step 9); confidence modulates how much scrutiny the memo recommends, never whether a human reviews it.
- **Memo rendering — two calls, not one, on purpose.** Native Citations and `output_config.format` structured outputs can't be combined on the same request — a real Anthropic API constraint we design around rather than discover live on stage. So there is exactly one LLM call for the memo: the citation-grounded narrative call above, plain-text output, which the API itself already splits into text-plus-citation blocks (`cited_text`, `document_title`, `page_location`) — not one string we'd have to regex apart. Backend code then assembles the JSON envelope (figures, status, confidence-breakdown) around that cited prose, because every one of those fields is already a tool output — the calculator, the precedence resolver, the confidence formula — not something a second model call needs to structure. The LLM writes the cited sentences; code owns the schema. The frontend still renders from one validated envelope — it's just the backend filling it in, not the model.
- **Demo-speed:** **prompt caching** on the ~100-page base agreement and the ~15-page amendment (cache both frozen document prefixes) so repeated reasoning/retrieval steps are fast and cheap; the scenario's retrievals are pre-warmed so the live take is ~15–20s.
- **Infra:** one Vultr Compute instance running Docker Compose (FastAPI + Next.js), talking to a separate **Vultr Managed Database** instance for Postgres + pgvector (a provisioned service, not a container we hand-roll) and **Vultr Object Storage** for the source PDFs (base agreement, amendment, financial exhibits). Locked in on day-one morning so infra never blocks us.

**The five tools (frozen schema, day one):**
1. `search_credit_agreement(query, corpus)` → semantic search scoped to `"base"`, `"amendments"`, or `"all"` over clause-chunked text in pgvector; returns clause text + document title + effective date + section + page.
2. `search_financials(borrower, period, line_item)` → structured lookup over the financial-statement tables.
3. `calculate_ratio(definition_version, inputs)` → **deterministic Python**; computes Net Leverage / coverage ratios from raw line items under a specified, named definition version. Zero LLM math.
4. `search_transactions(borrower, period, filter)` → structured GL lookup; the follow-up retrieval that confirms a driver's dollar amount and category actually qualify under whichever definition controls.
5. `notify_stakeholder(memo)` → fires a real Slack webhook + returns the rendered memo.

**Agentic control flow (explicit state machine, manual tool loop):** PLAN (which covenants; which defined terms have amendment history and need a version check) → for each covenant { RETRIEVE base agreement (definition + carve-out) → RETRIEVE financials → TOOL: calculate_ratio (base definition) → DECIDE status + cushion% + compare to self-reported figure → **IF `|recomputed − self-reported| > 0.02x`** (a small fixed tolerance — enough to absorb rounding, nowhere near enough to hide a redefinition): RETRIEVE amendments for the same defined term → RESOLVE precedence (deterministic) → RETRIEVE transactions to confirm the driver qualifies under the controlling definition → RECOMPUTE pro-forma } → SYNTHESIZE memo → HUMAN approve/override → notify. The loop checks `stop_reason == "tool_use"`, executes the tool, feeds the `tool_result` back, and repeats under a hard step cap.

**State / memory:** the agent carries a working state object across steps — the covenant checklist (with a per-covenant flag for "has amendment history"), the retrieved clauses tagged by document and effective date (so the precedence step and the memo can both reference them without re-retrieving), computed ratios under each definition version, discrepancies, and the evidence chips streamed to the UI.

**Trust architecture (borrowed from the healthcare brainstorm, applies verbatim here):** when a required line item — or a required amendment's effective-date confirmation — is missing, the agent surfaces **UNKNOWN with the missing datum named** — it never forces a guess, on either the math or the precedence question. A false "compliant" that hides a breach, a false "breach" that's actually cured by an amendment nobody checked, or a confident precedence call the agent can't actually support, are all worse than an honest "needs human review."

# 8. The 1-minute demo script — exactly what judges see, moment by moment

- **0:00–0:05 —** Portfolio view: one row, "Meridian Logistics," flashes **AMBER**. Narration (one line, timed to actually fit five seconds): *"A credit agreement isn't one document — it's a stack of amendments."* Analyst clicks the row.
- **0:05–0:22 —** The trace (left pane) ticks through, an evidence chip landing as each step completes:
  - PLAN: *"3 covenants tested. Net Leverage's EBITDA definition has amendment history — flagging for a version check."* (The other two covenants show a quick greyed "no amendment on file — skipping version check" chip: the branch is visibly conditional, not scripted.)
  - RETRIEVE base agreement: *"Net Leverage — §7.1, p.42"* + *"Consolidated EBITDA (original) — p.94."*
  - RETRIEVE financials: *"Q3 line items pulled."*
  - TOOL `calculate_ratio`: *"Net Leverage (base definition) = 4.08x."*
  - DECIDE: threshold 4.00x → **BREACH on the base definition** (amber → red). Gotcha chip: *"Borrower self-reported 3.95x — discrepancy +0.13x. Checking our own assumptions before the borrower's math."*
- **0:22–0:40 —** CONDITIONAL branch fires → RETRIEVE amendments: *"First Amendment to Credit Agreement, filed [date] — Consolidated EBITDA redefined, p.6."* RESOLVE precedence (deterministic, shown on screen as a filing-date comparison, not a model opinion): *"Amendment postdates the base agreement, not superseded → this definition controls."* RETRIEVE transactions: *"Scanning Q3 GL… $4.2M one-time legal settlement — qualifies under the amended add-back basket."* RECOMPUTE: *"Pro-forma Net Leverage = 3.88x → WATCH, not BREACH — gap to self-report narrows from 0.13x to 0.07x."*
- **0:40–0:52 —** The memo (right pane) writes itself, each figure carrying a citation pill tagged with its source document. We click **"4.08x"** → the source financial cell highlights. We click **"First Amendment, p.6"** → it jumps to and highlights page 6 of a visibly *different* PDF than the base agreement. The confidence breakdown shows: **92** (coverage 100 / calc-match 98 / precedence 75). This is the "prove it's real" beat.
- **0:52–0:58 —** Analyst clicks **Approve & Escalate.** A real **Slack** message fires on screen: *"Meridian Logistics — Net Leverage WATCH (amended EBITDA definition applied), memo attached."*
- **0:58–1:00 —** Tagline card: *"Covenant Chain — catches the amendment a rushed analyst forgot to re-check. Hours to seconds, every number and every document version cited."*

**Live challenge we invite:** *"Judges, pick any number in the memo, or challenge which document controls — we'll show you the source and the re-derivation for either."* Because the ratio is deterministic arithmetic and the controlling document is a checkable filing-date comparison, we can answer any challenge on the spot — a harder-to-fake live-challenge hook than arithmetic alone. This is why this build wins the Demo criterion (50%).

# 9. Impact & adoption

- **Real-world grounding:** a named buyer (covenant/credit-risk analysts at private-credit funds and bank leveraged-lending desks), a budgeted pain (firms already pay Covenant Review / Reorg to track exactly this kind of amendment history), and an hours-to-seconds efficiency story a portfolio-risk team would actually deploy. Missing a covenant breach costs the lender real money; missing that a breach is already cured by an amendment costs the *borrower* real money — an unnecessary default notice, a needless waiver fee. Continuous, version-aware monitoring is directly on-spec for the brief's Finance example, one layer past its literal wording.
- **Failure modes handled:** (1) zero LLM arithmetic — a deterministic tool re-derives every ratio; (2) zero LLM judgment on which document controls — a deterministic filing-date comparison, not a model opinion; (3) **UNKNOWN surfaced with the missing datum named** — never a forced guess, on either the math or the precedence question; (4) human-in-the-loop — the agent flags and defers, it does not autonomously declare default; (5) every claim cited to a source page in its actual source document — the direct answer to the black-box adoption failure that sank IBM Watson for Oncology; (6) confidence rendered as a defined, weighted breakdown, not an opaque or self-reported number.
- **Who benefits:** credit-risk analysts (hours back per quarter, and protection from the specific, expensive mistake of working off a superseded definition), portfolio managers and credit committees (an audit-ready memo they can act on), borrowers (fewer false-breach notices from a lender working off stale terms), and the institution (real breaches caught before they trip a default, false alarms caught before they trip an unnecessary one).

# 10. Risks & mitigations; stretch goals

**Risks & mitigations:**
- *Over-scoping into a platform (multi-borrower, trend charts, integrations) that ends up broad and fragile.* → Freeze to one borrower / one base agreement + one amendment / three covenants by Day 1 midday; treat every new feature as blocked until the single path runs end-to-end unattended.
- *No single real EDGAR filer's numbers land inside the exact near-breach band the demo needs.* → Day 0 morning is dedicated entirely to sourcing: a company that filed both a Credit Agreement exhibit and a later amendment redefining EBITDA is a searchable, well-populated pattern on EDGAR full-text search, not a rare find; if the numbers still don't land, calibrate 1–2 line items on the real document structure and disclose it (§7) rather than force a fabricated document.
- *Live tool-call flakiness or latency killing the 60-second demo.* → Freeze the tool schema early; happy path working by end of Day 1; pin/cache the scenario's retrievals and pre-warm the vector index + prompt cache; hard step cap; keep a recorded backup clip.
- *The citation-highlight jump misbehaves live for the amendment citation specifically* — a second, different PDF makes this fiddlier than a single-document jump, and it's the demo's proof-it's-real beat. → Pre-render the exact highlighted-page image for each of the 2–3 citations that appear on camera and swap it in if live span-mapping is slow or wrong; visually identical, zero live-rendering risk on the beat the climax depends on.
- *A slow pipeline reading as "slides with extra steps."* → Design every waiting moment to itself look like agent progress (trace ticking, chips populating, document-title badges switching), and hard-cap the live run at ~15–20s.
- *Retrieval missing the buried carve-out, or missing that an amendment exists at all.* → A real borrower confirmed on Day 0 to have both documents on file + clause-level chunking scoped by corpus + rehearse the exact path repeatedly.
- *Framework magic breaking live.* → Hand-rolled explicit state machine, no orchestration framework.

**Stretch goals (only after the happy path is frozen):**
- A second, later amendment on the same defined term (Amendment No. 2) — proves precedence resolution handles a real two-hop chain, not just one.
- A second borrower or a second covenant type (Interest Coverage).
- A cushion-over-quarters trend chart to show deterioration velocity.
- A live "Request More Data" branch that emails the borrower for a missing line item.
- Multi-quarter memory to detect not just breach but the *rate* of erosion.
