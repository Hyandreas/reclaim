## Per-axis reasoning

### Novelty — 3.5 / 5

The core concept is, by the proposal's own honest admission, the track brief's named Finance example ("covenant-monitoring agent with an escalation memo + confidence score"). That caps the ceiling here — this is not a concept the room hasn't seen. What lifts it above a plain 3 is a genuine, well-argued insight layered on top: a credit agreement is not one static PDF but a *stack of separately-filed amendments that silently redefine the terms a ratio depends on*, so the harder retrieval problem is "which document is even authoritative?" rather than "find the clause." That is a real, non-obvious differentiator — most teams will build the carve-out lookup and treat the agreement as one file. The choreography that makes it agentic is also sharp: the second retrieval is triggered by a *numeric discrepancy the viewer sees on screen before the retrieval it causes*, which is the single most legible way I've seen to demonstrate "retrieves more than once when it needs to" rather than assert it. The "we can defend our bibliography, not just our arithmetic" framing and the deterministic precedence resolver (code, not the LLM, decides which document controls) are memorable and conceptually clean. But the insight is an incremental — if smart — push past the obvious, not a second-order surprise. The single base→amendment hop is the *minimum viable* version of the precedence idea; the version that would be strikingly original (a real multi-hop chain where a superseded amendment must be rejected, or one redefinition propagating across multiple covenants) is parked in stretch goals. It's a solid concept with a genuine twist, which is 3.5.

### Implementation Plan — 4.5 / 5

This is close to exemplary and is the strongest part of the proposal. The stack is concrete and honors the track-mandated technology thoroughly: Next.js + SSE frontend, Python/FastAPI backend with a deliberately hand-rolled state machine (not LangGraph — with a defensible rationale), Claude Opus 4.8 via the Messages API with specific params, pgvector on Vultr Managed Database, Vultr Object Storage for PDFs, Docker Compose on Vultr Compute. The agentic flow is fully specified as an explicit loop with a hard step cap, five frozen-schema tools, and a concrete data-triggered branch (`|recomputed − self-reported| > 0.02x`). The single best decision is pushing both the arithmetic (`calculate_ratio`) *and* the precedence call (filing-date comparison) into deterministic code — "if a fact is objectively checkable, code decides it, not the LLM" — which makes the demo defensible under live challenge. §7's real/curated/cached breakdown is the most honest scoping I've seen: it names exactly what's genuine (real EDGAR filings, the amendment relationship, the tool calls, the Slack fire) and exactly what's tuned (1–2 dollar line items on real document structure) without hiding it. Risk handling in §10 is specific and each risk has a real mitigation, including a pre-rendered highlighted-page fallback for the trickiest beat. The team even designs around a real API constraint (native Citations vs. structured output on one call) rather than discovering it live. What keeps it from a 5: three small-but-real soft spots. (1) The confidence formula's `calc-match` sub-score (30% weight) rewards *proximity to the borrower's self-reported number* — but the entire thesis is that the counterparty can be wrong (working off a stale definition); a correct agent that legitimately diverges from a wrong borrower would be *penalized* on confidence, which is backwards. (2) The "pick any number" live-challenge invitation is in slight tension with the disclosed calibration of 1–2 line items — a judge who picks a calibrated input and asks "show me that in the 10-Q" hits the one soft spot. (3) The two-PDF citation highlight is the climax and the fiddliest UI piece; the pre-rendered fallback covers only the 2–3 on-camera citations, so an off-script judge click during the invited live challenge could miss. All three are addressable, and a capable team clearly ships this — but they are genuine gaps.

### Usefulness / Relevance — 5 / 5

Dead-on the track statement and clearly valuable. It is web-based, grounds every decision in documents, plans, retrieves more than once (base → amendments → transaction ledger, with the 2nd and 3rd retrievals genuinely data-triggered), calls tools, makes real decisions (BREACH/WATCH/UNKNOWN, precedence resolution, human deferral), and ends in an outcome an enterprise team could act on directly (audit-ready cited escalation memo + defined confidence score + real Slack escalation) — in Finance. The user is named precisely (covenant/credit-risk analyst at a private-credit fund or bank leveraged-lending desk), the pain is articulated with unusual specificity (computing against a superseded definition, which "looks like an ordinary quarterly pass," which is why it survives review), and the market is evidenced by real paid incumbents (Covenant Review, Reorg) that prove both buyer and budget. The amendment-precedence angle doesn't just satisfy the brief — it makes the tool *more* useful than the literal example by catching a specific, expensive, bidirectional failure mode (false breach costs the borrower a needless waiver fee; missed breach costs the lender). The narrow demo scope (one borrower, one amendment, three covenants) is the right hackathon cut and doesn't diminish the concept's relevance.

## Concrete strengths

1. **Deterministic where it matters, model where it must be.** Ratio math and the precedence call are both code, not LLM — the LLM only interprets carve-out language and drafts prose. This is the correct division and it makes the demo bulletproof to the two most obvious judge challenges.
2. **The agentic branch is demonstrated, not asserted.** The discrepancy appears on screen *before* the retrieval it triggers — the clearest RAG-vs-agent proof in a live demo, and it directly satisfies the track's "retrieve more than once when needed."
3. **Honesty in scoping (§7).** Real/curated/cached is labeled explicitly. This builds credibility rather than eroding it, and it's exactly what the rubric rewards.
4. **A real conceptual insight.** "Re-verify which document is authoritative before trusting what you read" is a genuinely different retrieval problem from carve-out lookup, and it's the mistake a rushed analyst is most likely to make.
5. **Demo design that pre-empts "staged."** The three-scenario plan — a clean no-amendment covenant (branch plans itself away), the flagged covenant, and a hard-breach case with no cure — proves the branch is conditional and that BREACH is reachable, not foregone.
6. **Beat-by-beat 60-second script (§8) with a live-challenge hook.** The demo is tightly choreographed and timed, and the "pick any number or challenge which document controls" invitation is a strong Demo-criterion play (worth 50%).
7. **Deep platform awareness.** Prompt caching for demo speed, SSE for live rendering, native Citations with `page_location`, and designing around the Citations-vs-structured-output constraint all signal a team that knows the stack.

## Weaknesses / gaps

1. **Novelty is capped by being the brief's own example.** The precedence layer is good but incremental; nothing forces a genuinely surprising second-order outcome. The most impressive version of the core idea (a multi-hop amendment chain) is a stretch goal, not the spine.
2. **The `calc-match` confidence sub-score has backwards semantics.** Rewarding proximity to the borrower's self-reported figure penalizes a *correct* agent that rightly diverges from a borrower computing on a stale definition — which is the product's entire reason to exist.
3. **"Pick any number" vs. calibrated inputs.** The invited live challenge is in tension with the disclosed tuning of 1–2 dollar line items; a judge probing a calibrated figure against the actual 10-Q finds the seam.
4. **Two-PDF citation highlight is the climax and the biggest live risk.** The pre-rendered fallback only covers rehearsed citations, so an unrehearsed judge click could break the "prove it's real" beat.
5. **Ambitious surface for 2 days.** Two-PDF clause chunking + corpus-scoped pgvector + five tools + SSE trace + citation highlighting across two documents + confidence breakdown + Slack + human-in-the-loop + three rehearsed scenarios is a lot; the whole thing hinges on Day-0 EDGAR sourcing landing on schedule.
6. **Degraded-state UX under the step cap is unspecified.** The hard step cap and hand-rolled loop are mentioned, but what the SSE trace shows if a tool stalls or the budget is exhausted mid-run isn't described.

## Prioritized fixes (highest score leverage first)

1. **(Novelty, biggest lever) Promote the two-hop amendment chain to the demo spine.** Make the controlling definition the one in *Amendment No. 2*, which supersedes *Amendment No. 1* on the same term, with a stale/superseded amendment the resolver must *reject*. A single base→amendment hop is the minimum viable version of your insight; a real chain where precedence has to pick the controller from three candidates is the version incumbents (Covenant Review) actually sell — and it makes the deterministic resolver's value undeniable. This is the change most likely to move Novelty from 3.5 to 4.5.
2. **(Novelty) Add a second-order twist the carve-out builds can't have.** Best option: reveal that the borrower's self-reported 3.95x was itself computed on a *third* (wrong/stale) definition, flipping the memo's conclusion from "we were wrong" to "they're on the wrong document — here's the one that controls." Alternatively, propagate the one EBITDA redefinition across two covenants (Net Leverage *and* Interest Coverage) to show one amendment cascading. Either turns a refinement into a genuine insight.
3. **(Implementation) Fix the `calc-match` semantics.** Reframe it as "residual explained" — how much of the *initial* discrepancy the agent accounted for via the controlling amendment + qualifying GL driver — rather than raw proximity to the self-report. Otherwise a correct agent that legitimately diverges from a wrong borrower is penalized exactly when it should be most confident. This closes a real logical hole in a component you already specify with a worked example.
4. **(Implementation) Resolve the live-challenge / calibration tension.** Either (a) spend more of Day 0 hunting for a borrower whose *real* numbers land in-band so zero calibration is needed and the challenge is truly bulletproof, or (b) scope the on-camera "pick any number" invitation to the deterministically-derived figures (the ratio, the filing-date comparison) — which are 100% defensible — and state plainly that raw dollar inputs are illustrative. Right now the invitation over-promises against §7's disclosure.
5. **(Implementation) Harden the two-PDF citation highlight against off-script clicks.** Either make live span-mapping robust enough to survive any citation in the memo, or pre-render highlighted pages for *every* citation the memo can contain (not just the 2–3 rehearsed), so the invited "pick any number" challenge can't break the climax beat.
6. **(Implementation, minor) Specify the degraded state.** Add one line on what the SSE trace renders if a tool exceeds the step budget or the model stalls — an explicit "needs human review / step cap reached" chip reinforces the trust story and removes a live-failure unknown.
